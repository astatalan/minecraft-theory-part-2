What do we mean by ‚Äúconsumer cooperative‚Äù in a gacha game?

A consumer cooperative is an entity owned and governed by its consumers (in this case, the players). Members/shareholders are players; they have voting rights or governance power; profits (or surplus from income after cost) are shared or reinvested in ways decided by the membership.

In the case of a gacha game, that means players have a say in certain decisions (how drop rates work, what content is released, how revenue is used), and perhaps receive part of the revenue or benefits from surplus.

Basic design of a cooperative gacha game

Here is a sketch of how it might function:

Component	Description
Ownership / governance	Players buy into membership (maybe via a one-time fee, or subscription, or contributions, or simply opt-in by playing). They elect a board (or use direct democracy) to make big decisions (e.g. content roadmap, monetization policies, drop rates, revenue allocations).
Revenue sources	Standard gacha purchases (real money, IAPs) + optional subscriptions or coop dues. Ad revenue, merchandising, etc.
Costs	Development, servers, art, outreach/marketing, support, continuous content updates.
Decision areas	Among others: what the drop rates are; whether ‚Äúpity‚Äù systems exist; transparency of odds; special banner events; how excess revenue is used (profit sharing, reinvestment in content, discounts, etc.).
Profit / surplus allocation	After operating costs, surplus is handled per cooperative rules: could be (a) dividends to members, (b) discounts or cashback, (c) reinvestment in game improvements, (d) shared special content or rewards for members, etc.
Key features/incentives

Transparency: members want clarity on drop rates, probabilities, what the revenue is being used for. Because they are owners, there‚Äôs an incentive to maintain trust.

Fairer monetization: less predatory mechanics (whales, pressure to spend) might be reduced or governed tightly. For example, minimal gambling-like behavior, guarantee systems.

Shared downside/upside: if revenues fall, players might accept lower frequency of new content rather than sharp push for monetization. If revenue does well, players benefit.

Governance participation: members vote on things like new characters, rate of content release, pricing, etc. This makes the game more aligned with community values.

A sample scenario (‚Äúday in the life‚Äù of this cooperative)

The cooperative launches with a founding membership. Each member gets one share, voting rights.

Developers propose a new banner: new characters, with certain drop rates. The proposal includes cost to develop, the expected IAP revenue, and the share of surplus to be returned to coop members. The members vote to accept or amend.

When players make gacha pulls, some portion of those revenues goes into a ‚Äúmember surplus pool.‚Äù Perhaps at each quarter, surplus is divided among members (or given as in-game credit).

Members can also propose initiatives (e.g. better pity system, or donation of part of revenue to charity) via democratic mechanisms.

Because members are players, they care about game fairness, so might push for mitigations of exploitative monetization features (e.g. very low drop rates, aggressive limited time exclusives) to maintain long-term health rather than short-term profit.

Trade-offs, risks, and challenges

Free rider problem: Some players will not buy in or contribute much, but will enjoy benefits of governance or surplus anyway. May need membership thresholds.

Decision-making bottlenecks: If votes are too frequent or too many issues, things slow down. Might need representative governance, committees, or delegated votes.

Revenue pressure: Gacha games traditionally rely on ‚Äúwhales‚Äù (players who spend a lot). A co-op model may reduce ability to extract high spending unless members approve it, possibly lowering revenue. That could mean less content or slower updates.

Incentives to increase spending: There's risk that members push for features that increase spending even if it harms player welfare (addictive mechanics), so need rules / guardrails.

Legal / financial complexity: Cooperatives are legal entities; revenue sharing may have tax implications; sharing digital goods vs real money; cross-jurisdiction complications (if players are in many countries).

Hybrid model: A for-profit company that issues a co-op arm or membership, but retains ownership over IP etc. Or a ‚Äúcooperative license‚Äù where community shareholders get part of revenue, but company handles development.

AI Overview
No human has ever given birth in a centrifuge. A patent for a "centrifugal birth table" was filed in 1963 and granted in 1965, but the device was never actually built or used for childbirth. It was a widely ridiculed idea considered extremely unsafe. 
The Blonsky device
The invention: The "Apparatus for Facilitating the Birth of a Child by Centrifugal Force" was patented by George and Charlotte Blonsky, who had no medical background.
How it worked (theoretically): A pregnant woman would be strapped to a spinning table, with her head at the center of the rotation. When labor began, the table would be spun at high speed, supposedly using centrifugal force to help deliver the baby into a net below.
Why it was a bad idea: The proposed rotational forces could reach up to 8 g‚Äîgreater than what a fighter jet pilot typically experiences‚Äîand would be incredibly dangerous for both the mother and baby.
The outcome: The Blonsky device was never built for medical use and was only constructed as a model for a museum exhibit years later. 
The dangers of hypergravity on pregnancy
Outside of the absurd Blonsky device, subjecting a pregnant person to a centrifuge would be extremely harmful. Studies on pregnant mice exposed to hypergravity show negative effects. The human body is evolved for life at 1 g, and higher levels of gravity can cause significant fluid imbalances and cardiovascular issues. In a modern medical setting, such an experiment on a pregnant human would never be approved due to the extreme risks.

What real science says

Photofission is a real process: heavy nuclei (like uranium, thorium, etc.) can absorb a high-energy gamma ray and undergo fission. 
Wikipedia

There is also photodisintegration (similar but instead of full fission you remove neutrons, protons or alpha particles), which plays a role in astrophysical nucleosynthesis (especially in explosive environments). 
DDE Scholar

The r-process (rapid neutron capture) is thought to produce many heavy elements, particularly beyond iron, in environments like neutron star mergers, some supernovae, and possibly in jets from collapsing massive stars. 
SciTechDaily

arXiv
+4

A very recent proposal: gamma-ray bursts (GRBs) might produce heavy elements via jets that generate free neutrons through interactions with high-energy photons. These neutrons could then feed r-process nucleosynthesis. 
SciTechDaily

So in real astrophysics, photofission and photon-induced reactions are part of how heavy nuclei can be broken up or transformed, and r-process is how heavier elements are built up. But the idea that photofission is the mechanism for producing life-essential elements (say carbon, nitrogen, etc., or maybe trace metals) is not something I've found in fiction or in current scientific speculation in exactly that role.

Why it might not appear in fiction (much)

The scientific plausibility is challenging: heavy element photofission requires very high gamma ray flux, specific conditions, and tends to produce heavy fragments rather than the lighter ones that biology uses.

Typically in worldbuilding, life elements (C, H, O, N, P, S, etc.) are assumed to come from ‚Äúnormal‚Äù stellar nucleosynthesis and supernovae. So author‚Äôs don‚Äôt usually need to invoke something as exotic as photofission for that, unless they want very ‚Äúhard‚Äù or unusual settings.

## What real science / theory touches on similar ideas

Here are relevant real or theoretical findings that are analogous or helpful:

* **Hawking radiation**: small (especially primordial or ‚Äúmorsel‚Äù) black holes are predicted to emit Hawking radiation, with a temperature inversely proportional to mass ‚Äî smaller mass ‚áí hotter, more energetic emissions. ([Science Focus][1])
* **Black hole ‚Äúmorsels‚Äù**: recent research (e.g. Cacciapaglia et al.) suggests that during black hole mergers, some small, asteroid-mass black holes (‚Äúmorsels‚Äù) might get ejected, and these could emit significant gamma rays (TeV range) via Hawking radiation. ([arXiv][2])
* **Primordial black holes and particle production**: Theoretical work shows that as primordial black holes evaporate, they could produce a broad spectrum of particles (whatever is allowed by physics) via Hawking radiation. ([Physical Review Journals][3])
* **r-process from primordial black holes**: There's very recent work (2025) about primordial black-hole formation & heavy r-process element synthesis tied to early Universe transitions. That suggests heavy elements could be produced in exotic environments associated with PBHs. ([SpringerLink][4])

So there *is* groundwork: small/evaporating BHs as sources of high energy photons and particles, which in some contexts might contribute to nucleosynthesis (creating or transforming heavy elements).

But photofission (i.e. gamma rays causing fission of existing heavy nuclei) is a different mechanism than heavy element *generation from scratch*. It requires having heavy nuclei around to be broken apart, plus enough gamma flux, etc. I didn‚Äôt find any astrophysics paper saying that photofission driven by Hawking radiation is a major contributor of life-elements (like carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, etc.).

## Possible Worldbuilding Scenarios / Concepts

Here are some ideas for how to build a setting around this concept:

1. **Black Hole Forge Zones**
   Regions (maybe deep in crusts or near asteroid belts) where many small BHs are anchored in fixed orbits. They emit gamma rays, photofission heavy element ore, liberating medium-mass nuclei. Those fragments are captured in a mineral matrix, then processed for life, or directly integrated. Maybe particular plants or fungi have evolved to use the gamma flux and the fission fragments as nutrient sources (metals, phosphorus, etc.).

2. **Civilization that Cultivates MicroBHs**
   A tech civilization has mastered creating, maintaining, and distributing small black holes. They use them as radiation reactors: the gamma rays induce photofission of heavy isotopes kept in ‚Äúreactor vessels.‚Äù The fission generates lighter atoms needed for fertilizer, medicine, metal extraction, etc. Maybe they even engineer ‚Äúfission farms‚Äù around BHs suspended in space or inside engineered habitats.

3. **Life Evolved in Gamma Zones**
   On planets or moons with many primordial micro black holes embedded in the mantle or near the surface, life evolved in high radiation, using the constant gamma flux to do chemical reactions otherwise requiring sunlight. Heavy element photofission is part of geological cycles: heavy islands of uranium etc., gamma flux breaks them, elements cycle, life uses metals arising from that. Maybe life uses gamma photosynthesis (or radiolysis etc.), and metal ion catalysts come directly from fission fragment pools.

4. **Planetary Ecosystem with Zoning**
   Outer part of the crust / surface is highly radiative and harsh; only extremophiles. Beneath a certain depth, shielding lets life be safe but still able to harness the gamma flux diffused or via specialized structures. Maybe big living ‚Äúspores‚Äù or microbial mats near underground fissures that filter particles and concentrate fragments.

5. **Unique Biochemistry**
   Maybe life uses unusual heavy isotopes made via photofission. Or life forms incorporate unstable isotopes that decay in metabolisms, providing energy. Or maybe life uses radiation-driven redox chemistry, using the fission fragment ions directly.

If you are building this world, to make it feel believable (internally consistent), you should define:

* What mass range the small black holes are in (so you can compute Hawking temperature, spectrum of emitted photons, gamma flux, lifetime).

* What are the heavy elements available for photofission (their abundance, distribution).
* What kind of shielding or adaptation life uses to survive near gamma flux.
* The chemical pathways from photofission products to life-useful atoms (how fragments are captured, decay, transported).
* How civilization (if there is one) harvests or manages all this without destroying the ecosystem.
